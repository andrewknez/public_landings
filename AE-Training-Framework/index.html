Understand the Company

Learn what Covalent is, why it exists, and how to explain it simply before you ever explain it to a VP of Operations.

What Covalent does, in plain English

Picture a factory floor. Every job on that floor, running a machine, torquing a bolt, signing off on a part, needs someone who is trained and currently able to do it safely and correctly. Most factories still track this with paper checklists, binders, and messy spreadsheets. When a supervisor needs to know who can do what, they have to guess or go dig through paperwork.

Covalent replaces all of that paper and all of those spreadsheets with one live system. It keeps track of who is trained, who is qualified right now, who is about to expire, and who is actually available on shift, all updated in real time.

The one-liner. Use this any time someone asks "what does Covalent do?"

"We help industrial companies manage training, qualification, and assignment of work at scale. We replace paper, Excel and tribal knowledge with a modern system to track who's qualified to work on what."

Follow it with the proof: we ramp new operators about 30% faster and save each worker around 10 minutes of unproductive time per shift, which adds up to roughly $300K per line per year in savings. Covalent currently manages qualifications for 50,000+ workers across its customer base, and documented results in comparable environments include approximately 5% gains in shift output and reduced quality escapes.

How Covalent thinks about its building blocks

As an AE, you will be the one explaining these concepts to VPs, quality directors, and IT leads, often in the same call. You need to be able to move fluidly between a simple explanation and the technical version.

Objective
One single thing a person needs to learn or prove they can do. Think of it as one line on a checklist, for example "can this person safely run this machine." An Objective can require a video, a quiz, a certain number of supervised hours, or a trainer's sign-off before it counts as done. Objectives can be scoped tightly, down to a specific location, part, machine, or process, and can require role-based sign-off, so only a Level 3 technician might be allowed to sign off a Level 1 Objective, for example.
Objective Group
A bundle of Objectives, like a folder holding several checklist items together. For example, "Certified Level 2 Machinist" might bundle five separate Objectives. The status of the parent group is derived entirely from the status of its children. If even one item inside expires or was never completed, the whole folder shows as "not qualified" until it's fixed. Objectives and Objective Groups can also be chained into prerequisites and structured pathways, for example Level 1 must be complete before Level 2 can start.
Skills Matrix
The master scoreboard. A real time, filterable grid where the rows are people and the columns are Objectives or Objective Groups, color coded green, yellow, or red (a "stoplight" view), so a supervisor can look at one screen and instantly see who is ready for what across a department, shift, or entire site.
Process Control (gating)
A guardrail that plugs into the customer's MES and physically blocks or flags an unqualified worker from being assigned to or signing off on a task. This is the capability that turns Covalent from a nice-to-have training system into an operational control that prevents bad outcomes before they happen.
Performance-based Proficiency
Being qualified is not a one time checkbox. Covalent uses live operational data, not just training completions, to know whether someone is still sharp. If someone hasn't actually performed a job in a defined window, they can be automatically flagged for a refresher or requalification, driven by an MES or similar integration.
Coverage Planning
A forward-looking staffing model. It answers "we have to hit an aggressive production target by a certain date, do we have the manpower, what's our biggest risk, and where do we need to cross-train or hire." This is a key value driver for Ops and Plant leadership.
Workforce Availability
Knowing not just who is qualified on paper, but who is actually clocked in right now (via a Time & Attendance integration), so a supervisor can plug a gap immediately instead of guessing.
Renewal
Like a license or certification, Objectives can expire and need to be redone on a schedule. Renewal logic can differ from the original Objective, for example a trainee needs 100 hours of OJT to originally earn an Objective, but only an annual supervisor observation to maintain it.
Audit trail and versioning
Every action taken on an Objective, by any user, is logged immutably with a digital signature and timestamp. Objectives themselves can be versioned over time, and prior versions remain visible so a regulator can see exactly what a worker was qualified against at any point in history.

Why Covalent exists

Manufacturers are losing their most experienced workers to retirement faster than they can train replacements. At the same time, customers like Boeing and regulators like the FAA and DCMA expect airtight, auditable proof that every worker on a job was actually qualified to do it. Paper and spreadsheets cannot keep up with either problem, and no single existing vendor category solves both training and real time operational enforcement together. Covalent exists to close that gap end to end.

Understand the Product

Develop true product depth. You need to run a credible demo, answer hard technical questions, and know when to loop in Product or Engineering.

The core workflow, start to finish

  1. A trainee is assigned one or more Objectives or Objective Groups, individually or in bulk, for example everyone in a location or business unit.
  2. The trainee works through the Objective: hosted or hyperlinked content, interactive checklists, logging units/hours/parts completed, and embedded evaluations that may be objective/programmatic or subjective in grading.
  3. Multiple stakeholders can be involved: trainee, trainer, evaluator, and admin, each with role based permissions.
  4. Once complete, the Objective (and any parent Objective Group) shows as qualified on the Skills Matrix in real time, filterable by people or Objective related attributes, and scopable by department and location.
  5. If configured, Process Control integration with the customer's MES gates or flags work if an unqualified person is assigned, and can recommend optimal assignments based on proficiency.
  6. Over time, Objectives require Renewal on their own expiration logic, and Performance-based Proficiency can auto-flag or auto-disqualify someone based on how recently they've actually worked the job, using MES or similar data.
  7. Everything is captured in an immutable audit trail with digital signatures, viewable by internal and external regulators on a read-only basis.

Advanced capabilities to know cold

  • Coverage Planning: models required resources against current qualifications to expose production risk and cross-training needs ahead of time.
  • Process Control: hard eligibility gating tied to badge-in or buy-off actions on the shop floor via MES integration.
  • Performance-based Proficiency: proficiency driven by actual work history, not just training completion, also requires MES-style integration.
  • Workforce Availability: filters Skills Matrices and reports by who's actually clocked in, via a Time & Attendance integration.
  • Pay-for-skills: Objective data can inform promotion or wage progression programs.
  • Bulk operations and API access: bulk import of people, Objectives, and historical proficiency data; API connections for Workday HR, Workday Learning, SSO, MES, and QMS systems.
  • Custom roles and views: configurable roles and default dashboards by role, with permission gated administration of Objectives.
You should be comfortable enough with these concepts to run a live demo, but always confirm complex or edge case technical questions (deep MES integration specifics, data migration scope, security architecture) with Product, Engineering, or your Sales Engineer rather than guessing.
Milestone check: Deliver a 15-minute product demo covering Objective assignment, the Skills Matrix, and at least one advanced capability, without a script.

Master the Messaging

Go beyond the elevator pitch into a full value narrative and business case you can defend in front of an executive.

The one-liner and proof, as your foundation

"We help industrial companies manage training, qualification, and assignment of work at scale. We replace paper, Excel and tribal knowledge with a modern system to track who's qualified to work on what."

Proof: ~30% faster operator ramp, ~10 minutes saved per operator per shift, ~$300K per line in annual savings, ~5% gains in shift output in comparable environments.

Building the business case

Where an SDR stops at the one-liner, you need to turn that into a quantified business case. Use the value workbook to translate the customer's own headcount, hourly cost, and production assumptions into a defensible ROI number, the way the Acme Corporation example in the MAP template shows ($1.2M annual labor efficiency gain across three sites, in that case). Always get the customer's finance or ops team to validate the underlying assumptions rather than presenting your own numbers as fact.

Call to action ladder for executive conversations

  1. "Can I send over a 2-minute video on how the platform works?" (lightest, useful for warming up a new stakeholder mid-cycle)
  2. "Would you be open to a quick benchmarking call, 20 minutes to compare how peers are handling this?"
  3. "How does [Company] currently manage whether a technician is qualified for a specific job?" (a question hook that surfaces the real pain)
  4. "We'll be in [city] next month implementing at [Boeing/GE site], worth grabbing 20 minutes while we're there?"
