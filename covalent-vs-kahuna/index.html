Where Kahuna is strong (plan around these)

Healthcare and clinical competency

Healthcare is Kahuna's origin and heaviest footprint. Clinical ladders, nursing competency checklists, Joint Commission readiness, and certification management are core territory. Covalent does not play in healthcare, so concede this ground rather than contest it.

HR, L&D, and Talent alignment

Kahuna is purpose-built for the skills-based-organization agenda: capability planning, demand-versus-supply scenarios, internal mobility, and career ladders. With an HR systems heritage and a Workday partnership, it lands naturally when HR or Talent owns the initiative.

Flexible competency modeling and assessment

Configurable frameworks with equivalencies, prerequisites, and multi-modal assessment (observations, quizzes, training, experience, certifications) are a recognized strength. Reviewers consistently praise the configurability and the implementation support.

Field service and energy mobility

For dispersed, mobile workforces in energy, oil and gas, and field service, the Maui mobile app offers offline use, shared-device support, and voice-assisted assessment. This is a genuine fit for environments without connectivity.

Category maturity and brand

Kahuna has built strong standing in the skills-management category, with analyst visibility, a Workday Marketplace presence, multilingual support, and an enterprise customer base. Expect a credible, well-supported competitor in committee evaluations.

Compliance and certification tracking

Mature certification management with automated reminders and audit-ready reporting across roles and sites. Strong wherever the buyer frames the problem primarily as regulatory compliance and competency assurance.

Where Covalent is strong (lead with these)

Acting on the data, not just reporting it

Covalent closes the loop from qualification to the floor. Coverage planning, job assignment, and skill-based work gating turn a skills record into the staffing and execution decision. Kahuna's output centers on the skills heatmap, development plans, and capability scenarios.

Built for complex discrete manufacturing and MRO

Covalent is purpose-built for the industrial frontline: jet engines, powersports, heavy equipment, and repair shops. Named OEM proof (Boeing, GE Aerospace, Great Dane, Mercury Marine, Polaris, MasterCraft) speaks directly to the buyer's peers. Kahuna spreads its focus across healthcare, energy, and field service.

Task and equipment level OJT execution

Gated, multi-role validation workflows, digital sign-offs, and per-task qualification reflect how qualification actually happens on a line. Eligibility is granular, composite, and recency-based, and it ties directly to authorization to perform work.

Production coverage and key-person risk

Covalent maps the skills matrix to jobs, lines, and processes to expose coverage gaps, bottlenecks, and single points of failure in real time. This operational coverage view is native rather than an add-on report.

Job assignment and deployment

At the Enterprise tier, Covalent matches the right worker to the right job using eligibility, availability, and performance data, and supports coverage planning and training scheduling. This deployment layer is where Covalent extends past competency tracking.

Connection to production systems

Operator eligibility can feed MES and badging so that only qualified technicians can sign and close work. Covalent sits as the upstream qualification signal that production and work-order systems consume.

How to sell against them

Talk tracks that move the deal to our ground

  • Anchor on what happens after the gap is known. Ask: once the platform shows Line 3 is two qualified operators short on second shift, what actually makes the staffing move happen? Covalent turns that signal into coverage planning and assignment.
  • Lead with discrete-manufacturing and MRO depth. Put named OEM peers in front of an industrial buyer: Boeing, GE Aerospace, Great Dane, Mercury Marine, Polaris, MasterCraft.
  • Frame the on-the-floor OJT workflow. Gated multi-role sign-offs, per-task qualification, touch data, and requal tied to production cadence reflect how a line actually qualifies people.
  • Connect qualification to execution. Operator eligibility feeding MES and badging so only qualified techs can sign and close work is a story aimed straight at Quality and Operations.

Questions to plant for the buyer to ask Kahuna

  • Show real-time coverage versus target by role and line, as an operational planning view rather than a development report.
  • Walk through how work is assigned based on eligibility, availability, and performance. Is it optimization, or a manual call by the supervisor?
  • Share discrete-manufacturing and aerospace OEM references specifically, separate from healthcare and energy accounts.
  • Explain how operator eligibility flows into MES or work-order gating so unqualified people are blocked from signing work.

Where to avoid the fight

  • Do not contest healthcare. It is Kahuna's home turf and outside Covalent's scope.
  • Do not get pulled into a pure competency-modeling-depth contest with an HR or Talent buyer. Reframe the conversation around operations, coverage, and throughput, where Covalent leads.
  • Do not overclaim end-to-end or all-in-one. Present coverage, assignment, and gating as progress toward the operational vision, backed by what is live today.
When we win, when they win

Covalent tends to win when

  • Operations, Manufacturing, or a Plant Manager owns the budget and the decision.
  • The pain is qualification rigor, coverage gaps, key-person risk, or ramp speed that hits throughput.
  • The environment is complex discrete manufacturing or MRO with task and equipment level qualification.
  • The buyer wants to act on the data with assignment, coverage planning, and work gating.
  • The compelling event is an audit, a ramp, SME attrition, or a legacy or paper system sunset.

Kahuna tends to win when

  • HR, L&D, or Talent owns the initiative and the budget.
  • The goal is an enterprise skills strategy, internal mobility, or career and clinical ladders.
  • The organization is in healthcare, or a dispersed energy or field-service workforce.
  • The priority is deep, fully configurable competency modeling and certification compliance.
  • A Workday-centric HR stack is the anchor and skills data needs to live close to it.
Objection handling
"Kahuna is the established leader in skills management."
Agreed that Kahuna is strong as an HR-centric system of record for skills and competency, especially in healthcare. Covalent is built for the industrial frontline and carries the data past the skills matrix into operational deployment: coverage planning and assignment that protect throughput on the line.
"Kahuna says AI-driven skills tools do not work."
Covalent does not infer competency from an algorithm. The data is earned through validated OJT and task performance. AI is applied on top of that trusted data to optimize assignment and coverage, and those capabilities are optional Enterprise features rather than the foundation.
"We already run Kahuna for skills."
That can coexist. Covalent's wedge is the production-execution layer: real-time coverage, eligibility-based assignment, and MES gating, plus the on-the-floor OJT workflows for task and equipment qualification. Start where the operational gap is widest and the throughput case is clearest.
"Kahuna is more configurable for our competency framework."
Configurability matters most when the goal is modeling skills for HR. When the goal is qualifying people for specific tasks and equipment and then staffing the line, the question shifts to whether the platform can gate work and drive assignment. That is where Covalent is built to lead.
